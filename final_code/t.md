In this experiment, Pattern 2 (a low-contrast, perceptually comfortable flicker stimulus) produced a statistically significant increase in gamma-band power (35–45 Hz) compared to baseline (mean difference ≈ +10.57 dB, p = 0.038, moderate effect size ≈ 0.59, with confidence intervals excluding zero), indicating that the stimulus successfully engages gamma activity in the brain. However, this increase was not accompanied by strong evidence of narrowband entrainment at the target frequency of 40 Hz, as both the 40 Hz SNR and peak frequency shift were not statistically significant. Additionally, the binomial sign test showed that although a majority of trials (10/15) exhibited increased gamma power, this consistency was not strong enough to reach significance. Together, these results suggest that Pattern 2 does not produce precise phase-locked entrainment, but instead induces a broader, non-phase-locked increase in gamma activity. This pattern of results is consistent with induced gamma responses rather than classical steady-state visually evoked potentials (SSVEP), likely due to the reduced contrast, transparency, and temporal variability inherent in the stimulus design on a 60 Hz display. Importantly, this demonstrates that perceptually subtle and comfortable visual stimuli can still modulate neural activity in the gamma band, albeit in a more distributed and less frequency-specific manner compared to high-contrast flicker conditions.

Gamma power (35–45 Hz) was significantly higher during stimulation (mean difference ≈ +0.81 dB, p = 0.0024, large effect size ≈ 1.46), with all trials (9/9) showing an increase, as confirmed by a highly significant binomial test (p = 0.00195). This provides strong evidence that the stimulus reliably induces gamma activity. Additionally, broadband measures showed significant increases, with wide-band SNR (35–45 Hz) also significantly elevated (p ≈ 0.038), indicating that the effect is not limited to a single frequency but extends across the gamma band. However, evidence for precise narrowband entrainment at exactly 40 Hz was weaker: while there was a shift in peak frequency towards 40 Hz and a moderate increase in 40 Hz SNR, these effects did not reach statistical significance.
